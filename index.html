In 1907, the first fully synthetic plastic was created, and due to its properties of being a good insulator and being durable, it quickly became a material that was researched and invested in. When World War II started, the U.S. greatly expanded the plastic industry as it was necessary to make parachutes, helmet liners, body armor, and more. With World War II, plastic production increased by 300%.

The first plastic debris in the ocean was first apparent in the 1960s, and since then, the amount of plastic in the ocean has only increased. As the public became more aware of the plastic pollution issue, the plastics industry encouraged recycling as a solution in the 1980s. Still, to this day, the solution of recycling plastic only solves a fraction of the pressing problem.

So the question now is, what can we do to fight the growing, global issue that is plastic pollution?

In 2018, about ⅓ of U.S. plastic waste that was to be recycled was exported out of the country, and of those exports, 78% were sent to countries that have poor waste management. These countries don’t have the infrastructure or regulation to process and recycle the plastic waste that the U.S. exports yearly.

But why does the U.S. do this?

Jan Dell, the founder of Last Beach Clean Up - a nonprofit organization dedicated to tell the public facts about plastic pollution, tells us that “[The U.S. doesn’t] have factories to do it.” The U.S. is not capable of handling the amount of plastic waste that is generated every year, but despite the lack of facilities and resources, the plastic waste that is generated in the U.S. increased five-fold from 1980 to 2018.

Then, is it sustainable for the U.S. to continually rely on plastic waste exporting?

No.


Because of the disproportionate amounts of plastic waste that many countries in Asia have had import, many have been implementing policies that limit or complete bank plastic waste imports. Between 2010 to 2016, China imported on average 8 million tons of plastic from nations across the globe, with the U.S. being one of the main contributors, alongside Hong Kong and Japan. In the beginning, it was a win-win situation for China to import plastic, as they would be able to use the materials to create and manufacture goods, but with these benefits, the high amount of plastic waste led to many environmental repercussions.

In January 2018, China banned the imports of most plastics and other materials that were sent to China's recycling processors. This policy is called the National Sword or Green Sword policy. It banned the importation of certain types of solid waste and set strict limits of contamination on the recyclable materials. These limits were a 24-item list of materials that would be prohibited from being imported into China, including post-consumer plastic scrap, types of unsorted paper, types of used textiles, and more. In 2019, China added another 16 materials that were prohibited from being imported.

Following China’s decision, Vietnam, Malaysia, Thailand, and many other countries have implemented their own regulations and bans on plastic waste imports.

These new policy bans explains why we can see in Graph 2 that there is a drop from 8% recycled to only 5-6% recycled.

What should the U.S. do now?

Many states already have plastic ban policies in place already. States with these policies have proven that they work, that the amount of plastic that is generated and used by residents of that state are less than states without these plastic ban legislations.

Figure 6. Colored map of states that have implemented a plastic ban or tax, states that have enacted a preemptive ban, and states with no plastic ban legislations. Hover over or click different states to reveal more information.

To do our part in society, we can manage our plastic waste better by simply producing and using less. However, to address this issue at large, we need the government to intervene, implementing plastic bans or taxes across the country. The U.S. cannot rely only on other countries to solve the plastic waste problem, we need preventative measures of our own.
